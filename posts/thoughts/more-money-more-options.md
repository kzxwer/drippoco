Lately, I've been paying much more attention to investing in stocks. The reason is simple: watching the rapid advancement of AI has made me realize that I need to accumulate more capital.

Some people may think the idea that AI will replace jobs is exaggerated or unrealistic. Others may believe that their own jobs will remain safe no matter what happens. Whether those fears ultimately become reality or not, I believe it is important to prepare for that possibility.

At the moment, it is still difficult to build a profitable product entirely with AI and have AI maintain it without human involvement. We are currently in a phase where people are amazed by what AI can create, experimenting with it for fun and testing its capabilities. Meanwhile, AI companies are collecting enormous amounts of data from these interactions, identifying weaknesses, and continuously improving their models.

As a software developer whose primary work revolves around coding, I can feel the speed of AI's progress more directly than most people. Tasks that once required entire teams can now be completed by a single developer working alongside AI tools. It has become possible for me to run multiple AI agents simultaneously, assigning them different tasks while I focus on something else.

In the future, companies may no longer need large teams of developers. Instead, a small number of people who know how to manage AI agents and maintain their outputs may be enough to run entire businesses. If that future arrives, my response should not simply be to look for another software engineering job. I will need to build something new or create a sustainable cycle that allows me to support myself independently. And that kind of freedom usually requires money.

I feel that we may be approaching a major turning point—not just in my own life, but for society as a whole. We could be entering a transformation unlike anything we have experienced before. The standards by which people measure valuable skills may change dramatically.

As AI increasingly handles complex reasoning, calculations, and problem-solving, humans may gradually lose some of those abilities themselves. At the same time, advances in virtual experiences could change the balance between the physical world and the digital one. For people who already spend most of their day inside screens, how much room will remain for physical experiences, genuine emotions, meaningful communication, and independent thought?

Predicting a future that nobody has experienced before is extremely difficult. There are no historical precedents we can rely on.

If large numbers of people truly lose their jobs, where will they find income? If unemployment rises significantly, consumer spending will likely decline. And if consumption falls, suppliers and businesses will struggle as well.

Consider an extreme example. Suppose there are plenty of homes available for rent, but fewer and fewer people can afford to pay for them. Would landlords lower prices? Or would they continue demanding the same payments despite shrinking demand?

What could governments realistically do in such a situation? Forcing housing prices down would likely face strong resistance. On the other hand, simply expanding financial assistance programs could increase public debt or widen wealth inequality. After all, welfare payments may provide people with the means to survive, but they do not necessarily provide opportunities to work.

It is a difficult problem.

From an employer's perspective, if there is a way to operate a company with fewer employees and lower labor costs, there is little incentive not to use it. Most companies are unlikely to stop and consider what happens if everyone adopts the same strategy simultaneously and how that might affect society as a whole.

Recently, I watched a video discussing the "death spiral" that could emerge from AI-driven zero-click environments, where users receive answers directly from AI without ever visiting the original sources.

I don't think this phenomenon will be limited to search engines.

The same pattern could emerge throughout the broader economy if AI-driven labor replacement becomes widespread. As AI takes over more productive activities, the people and businesses that originally created value may receive less compensation. Over time, fewer creators may be willing or able to produce the content, services, and innovations that AI depends on. Eventually, the very system that AI relies upon could begin to weaken itself.

That is why I believe the discussion around AI is not simply about technology. It is about the future structure of the economy, the meaning of work, and how society will adapt when human labor is no longer the primary source of value creation.
